Flares from active galactic nuclei

Blazars are active galactic nuclei, which harbor a relativistic jet oriented towards (or nearly) the line of sight. They are classified as BL Lacertae (BL Lacs) or Flat Spectrum Radio Quasars (FSRQs), depending on their spectral features.

Blazar flares are extensively studied in the literature through the modeling of their multi-wavelength and multi-messenger emissions. These models require a significant number of parameters, and therefore focus on specific sources with high quality multi-wavelength and multi-messenger data, in order to break degeneracies in the parameter space (Gao et al. 2018, Keivani et al. 2018, Petropoulou et al. 2019, Oikonomou et al. 2019, Xue et al. 2019, Xue et al. 2020, Petropoulou et al. 2020). The models usually predict steady state spectra during the flare, potentially compared with quiescent spectra.

Gamma-ray bursts

Gamma-ray bursts (GRB) encompass various categories, involving different progenitors and source environments, and can be associated with successful to fully chocked jets, or winds, with numerous promising sites for HE-UHE neutrino production, such as internal shocks, or jet-cocoon interaction sites. Populations of sources hidden in terms of EM radiation could provide a significant contribution to the diffuse HE neutrino flux.

Long GRBs. Supernovae and hypernovae are closely connected to long GRBs and low-luminosity GRBs (LL GRBs) (Hjorth and Bloom 2011). Long GRBs are the most luminous sources of EM radiation of the Universe, produced by the core collapse of super-massive stars. Their precursor and prompt phases are dominated by gamma rays, whereas their afterglow is dominated by radio and X-ray emissions. Their production of HE to UHE neutrinos during these different phases has been extensively studied (Waxman and Bahcall 1997, Dermer 2000, Murase and Nagataki 2006, Murase 2007, Murase et al. 2008, Meszaros 2015, Globus et al. 2014), and depends strongly on the production sites (Pitik et al. 2021). Correlations with GeV-TeV neutrinos could help constrain GRB emission mechanisms (Murase et al. 2013). The detection of VHE emissions during GRB afterglows, for instance GRB 180720B or GRB 190114C (Abdalla et al. 2019, MAGIC collaboration 2020) challenges emission models and opens the possibility of an hadronic component required to explain the light curve.

Low luminosity and choked GRBs. LL GRBs have been suggested as a separate population from high-luminosity GRBs, due to their longer variability timescales, lower luminosities and break energies, and possibly lower Lorentz factors (Virgili et al. 2008, Bromberg et al. 2011). Unified models consider off-axis or semi-chocked jets (Nakar 2015, Salafia et al. 2016). However, off-axis models tend to be inconsistent with observations due to the lack of a bright afterglow. HE neutrino production is studied in the context of trans-relativistic shock breakout models or weak jets, with fully or partially choked jets depending on the properties of the progenitor (Kashiyama et al. 2012, Senno et al. 2015, Boncioli et al. 2018, Fasano et al. 2021).

Short GRBs and neutron star mergers. Short GRBs belong to a separate class of transient events, linked to compact-object binary mergers. The well known NS-NS merger GW170817/GRB-170817A has been detected in GW and across the full EM spectrum. Existing scenarios for the production of HE-UHE cosmic rays and neutrinos in short GRBs include for instance the production of a magnetar by the NS-NS merger (Piro and Kollmeier 2016, Fang and Metzger 2017), or structured jets interacting with the merger ejecta (Kimura et al. 2018, Gottlieb and Globus 2021). The production of transient HE neutrino signals from NS-NS merger remnants has also been studied (Decoene et al. 2019).

Tidal disruptions by massive black holes

Tidal disruptions of stars by massive black holes, also called Tidal Disruption Events (TDEs) have been observed in the UV, X-rays and gamma rays (Komossa 2015). They can produce luminous and long-lasting flares, also called Tidal Disruption Flares (TDFs), sometimes associated with relativistic jets (Bloom et al. 2011, Burrows et al. 2011).

HE-VHE neutrino production in TDE jets has been extensively studied (Murase 2008, Murase and Takami 2009, Wang et al. 2011, Wang and Liu 2015, Senno et al. 2016, Dai and Fang 2016, Lunardini and Winter 2016, Biehl et al. 2017, Guepin et al. 2017, Winter and Lunardini 2020), as well as models involving non-relativistic outflows and off-axis jets (Hayasaki and Yamasaki 2019, Murase et al. 2020, Liu et al. 2020), further motivated by the hint of association between AT2019dsg and IC191001A (Stein et al. 2020).

Some of these models account for possible acceleration and interactions of cosmic-ray nuclei (Biehl et al. 2017, Guepin et al. 2017), or account for time-dependent modeling (Guepin et al. 2017, Hayasaki et al. 2019, Winter and Lunardini 2020).

The production of HE to UHE neutrino from novae, thermonuclear or core-collapse supernovae, hypernovae and super-luminous supernovae has been extensively studied. Many studies focus on modeling their quiescent emissions, and mostly hadronic interactions, due to the dense hadronic backgrounds. However, transient HE neutrino emissions can be produced through interactions with non-thermal, thermal or baryonic fields of the ejecta.

A precise modeling of HE neutrino light-curve can help to identify transient or time-dependent emissions from these sources (Murase et al. 2009, Fang et al. 2015, Murase 2017, Fang et al. 2020).

The production of HE to UHE neutrino from these aource classes is closely related with the production of neutrinos from pulsars and magnetars (Beall and Bednarek 2001), as supernovae, hypernovae and super-luminous supernovae can be driven by pulsars or magnetars. Pulsars and magnetars have been suggested as potential VHE-UHE cosmic-ray sources, e.g. (Arons 2003, Fang et al. 2012, Kotera et al. 2015, Guepin et al. 2019), and can produce Galactic or extra-galactic transient neutrino emissions.

Neutron stars

Young pulsars or magnetars with millisecond periods could produce transient emissions associated with the short characteristic timescales of their rotation energy losses. Interaction with radiation backgrounds inside the pulsar magnetosphere could also lead to transient HE-UHE neutrino emissions. In existing magnetar models, protons extracted from the surface of young magnetars can be accelerated to ultra-high energies, and interact with the high X-ray background close to the surface or the surrounding baryonic background, potentially enhanced during/after bursts and flares (Zhang et al. 2003, Ioka et al. 2005).

Compact object mergers

Gravitational wave detections by the LIGO and Virgo collaborations have raised the interest for compact object mergers, mainly black hole (BH-BH) mergers, but also black hole-neutron star (BH-NS) mergers, and neutron star mergers previously mentioned. The existence of EM and MM counterparts to BH-BH or BH-NS has been proposed (Murase et al. 2016, Kotera and Silk 2016, deVries et al. 2016), for instance with the Blandford-Znajek process generating an outflow. White dwarf mergers have also been proposed as a source of high-energy neutrinos (Xiao et al. 2016), with a potentially optically thick debris disk hiding HE photons from the observer.

X-ray binaries

X-ray binaries (XRB) are composed of a neutron star or a black hole and a companion star. These sources are bright in X-rays and sometimes gamma rays, and can present winds or collimated beams (mircoquasars).

XRBs are interesting prospective Galactic sources for cosmic-ray acceleration and HE neutrino production (Levinson and Waxman 2001, Anchordoqui et al. 2002, Distefano et al. 2002, Baerwald and Guetta 2012, Sahakyan et al. 2013). Existing models consider photohadronic but also purely hadronc interactions. Moreover, they mostly focus on diffuse emissions, and predict neutrino signals principally below the HE range. Nevertheless, transient neutrino signals could be produced in coincidence with their periodic emissions and outbursts.
